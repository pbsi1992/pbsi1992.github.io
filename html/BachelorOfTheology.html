Bachelor of Theology Program

Goals and Objectives

only 118 total units for the span of 2 I/2 years. Field Ministry subjects are for reporting purposes only.


TRIMESTER 1

Theology 1: Introduction to key doctrines of the Christian faith, focusing on foundational theological concepts.
This course serves as an introduction to the essential doctrines of the Christian faith from a distinctly Reformed and confessional perspective. Emphasis is given to the theological method, the authority and sufficiency of Scripture, and the foundational doctrines of God, revelation, creation, providence, and man. The study is guided by the historic Reformed confessions (e.g., Westminster Standards, Three Forms of Unity), grounding students in biblically faithful and theologically coherent doctrine.

Church History 1: Study of the early church, major councils, and development of Christian doctrine up to the Reformation.
This course traces the development of the Christian church from the apostolic era to the eve of the Protestant Reformation. It highlights the work of the Holy Spirit in preserving the Church through times of persecution, doctrinal controversies, and institutional corruption. Students will explore key figures, church councils, creeds, heresies, and movements, analyzing how these historical developments shaped the formation of Christian doctrine and practice. A Reformed analysis will highlight God's providence in preserving gospel truth through church history.

Old Testament Survey: Overview of the Old Testament books, themes, and historical context.
This course offers a comprehensive overview of the Old Testament, highlighting its literary structure, historical background, theological themes, and covenantal unity. Students will be taught to view the Old Testament not merely as history, but as the unfolding redemptive plan of God, culminating in Christ. Special attention will be given to Reformed theological themes such as covenant, promise, typology, and the sovereignty of God in Israel's story.

Book of Acts: In-depth study of the Acts of the Apostles, focusing on the early church and missionary efforts.
This course provides an in-depth exegetical and theological study of the Acts of the Apostles, emphasizing the sovereign work of the Holy Spirit in the expansion of the early church from Jerusalem to the ends of the earth. The narrative is approached as a continuation of the redemptive-historical story begun in the Gospels, with a focus on the apostolic preaching, church planting, persecution, missionary journeys, and the fulfillment of Old Testament promises in the person of Christ. The Reformed understanding of God’s providence and the means of grace will be underscored throughout the course.

Pauline Epistles: Examination of the letters of Paul, their theological significance, and application.
This course examines the letters of the Apostle Paul, exploring their theological, pastoral, and practical teachings within the framework of covenant theology. The course highlights key Reformed doctrines such as justification by faith alone, union with Christ, election, sanctification, and the role of the church in the Christian life. Attention will also be given to the historical context of each letter and Paul's role as a divinely appointed apostle to the Gentiles.

Evangelism & Discipleship: Training in the principles and practices of sharing the gospel and nurturing believers.
Rooted in the Great Commission and the sovereignty of God in salvation, this course trains students in the biblical principles and practical methods of evangelism and discipleship. The Reformed perspective highlights God’s initiative in salvation, the use of the ordinary means of grace (Word, Sacraments, and Prayer), and the church’s role in nurturing mature disciples. Students will develop skills in sharing the gospel clearly and discipling believers within a covenant community context.

Field Ministry: Practical experience in ministry settings to apply learned concepts.
This hands-on course offers students guided ministry experience in church or mission settings to integrate theological learning with real-life service. Under the supervision of faculty and local church leaders, students will serve in teaching, outreach, worship, counseling, or administrative roles. The experience is guided by a Reformed understanding of vocation, church order, and ministry under the Lordship of Christ.

TRIMESTER 2

Theology 2: Continuation of systematic theology, covering doctrines like Christology, soteriology, and pneumatology.
This course continues the study of Systematic Theology by exploring the doctrines of Christ (Christology), salvation (Soteriology), and the Holy Spirit (Pneumatology) from a thoroughly Reformed and confessional perspective. It presents Christ as the Mediator of the Covenant of Grace, salvation as the work of the Triune God, and the Holy Spirit as the one who applies redemption to believers. Emphasis is placed on the sovereignty of God in salvation, union with Christ, and the ordo salutis (order of salvation).

Church History 2: Exploration of the Reformation, post-Reformation developments, and modern church history.
This course explores the historical development of the church from the Protestant Reformation to the modern era, emphasizing the sovereign hand of God in preserving and reforming His Church. Students will analyze the theological convictions of the Reformers, the rise of Reformed confessionalism, Puritanism, the Great Awakenings, and the challenges posed by modernism, liberalism, and secularism. The course highlights the importance of returning to sola scriptura and the gospel of grace.

New Testament Survey: Overview of the New Testament books, themes, and historical context.
This course provides an overview of the 27 books of the New Testament with a focus on their historical context, theological themes, literary structure, and their place in redemptive history. Students will see the New Testament as the fulfillment of the Old Testament promises, centered on the person and work of Christ. The survey will highlight each book’s contribution to the gospel message and to the life of the church.

Major Prophets: Study of the major prophetic books, their messages, and relevance.
This course is an in-depth study of Isaiah, Jeremiah, Lamentations, Ezekiel, and Daniel. The major prophets are interpreted through a covenantal and Christ-centered lens, with a focus on God's holiness, judgment, redemption, and restoration of His people. Students will explore the historical context, prophetic calling, theological themes, and messianic promises of each book, and their relevance for the church today.

English 1: Academic English skills, focusing on reading, writing, and critical analysis.
This foundational course develops students’ academic English skills in reading comprehension, critical thinking, and academic writing—essential tools for theological study. Students will analyze theological texts, construct logical arguments, and write coherent essays and reflection papers in preparation for higher academic tasks.

Psychology 101: an introduction to the scientific study of human behavior and mental processes, covering key topics such as perception, cognition, emotion, learning, and personality. The course also explores foundational theories, research methods, and practical applications in various fields, including clinical, social, and developmental psychology.
This course introduces the scientific study of human behavior and mental processes while integrating a biblical understanding of human nature. Topics include perception, cognition, emotion, learning, development, personality, and abnormal psychology. Students are encouraged to critique secular psychological theories in light of a biblical anthropology grounded in the image of God, the Fall, and redemption in Christ.

TRIMESTER 3

Theology 3: Further exploration of systematic theology, including ecclesiology, eschatology, and sacraments.
This course explores the biblical doctrines of the church (ecclesiology), last things (eschatology), and the sacraments, with an emphasis on their development in Reformed theology. Students will understand the nature, marks, and mission of the church, the Reformed view of the sacraments as means of grace, and a biblical framework of the end times. The covenantal structure of Scripture and the Reformed confessions serve as key interpretive guides.

Presbyterian History: Study the Presbyterian tradition's history and distinctives within the Reformed faith.
This course surveys the development of Presbyterianism as a key expression of Reformed Christianity. Beginning with the Reformation roots in Scotland and Geneva, students will examine key figures, confessions (especially the Westminster Standards), polity, and theological distinctives of Presbyterian churches. Special attention will be given to how Presbyterianism has shaped missions, education, and church government globally.

Minor Prophets: Examination of the minor prophetic books, their messages, and application.
This course offers an in-depth study of the twelve Minor Prophets (Hosea to Malachi), emphasizing their covenantal messages, call to repentance, themes of justice and mercy, and prophetic anticipation of the Messiah. Students will examine the historical context of each book and their continued relevance to the life of the church and society today. Christ-centered exegesis and pastoral application are key features of this course.

Greek 1: Introduction to New Testament Greek, focusing on grammar and vocabulary.
This course introduces students to the basic grammar, vocabulary, and syntax of New Testament (Koine) Greek. Emphasis is placed on developing foundational skills in reading, translating, and understanding the structure of the Greek New Testament. Students will begin translating simple texts and using Greek tools for deeper biblical interpretation.

English 2: Focuses on developing advanced reading, writing, speaking, and critical thinking skills through the study of literature, composition, and research. The course emphasizes analytical writing, grammar refinement, and effective communication, preparing students for academic and professional settings.
Furthermore, this course builds on foundational skills to further develop critical thinking, analytical writing, advanced reading comprehension, and oral communication. Through the study of literature and theological texts, students learn to construct well-reasoned arguments, write research papers, and present ideas clearly in academic and ministry contexts.

Foundation of Christian Education: Principles and methods for teaching Christian education.
This course explores the biblical foundations, theological principles, and practical methods of Christian education from a Reformed perspective. It examines how God's truth is taught across generations, the role of the church and family, and the integration of doctrine with discipleship. Emphasis is placed on Christ-centered curriculum design and teaching strategies for various age groups.

Music 1: Introduction to church music, its history, and its role in worship.
This course introduces the biblical, theological, and historical foundations of church music, especially in the Reformed tradition. It explores the role of music in worship, psalmody, hymnody, liturgical forms, and how music shapes the soul and theology of the church. Students will examine the regulative principle of worship and evaluate modern worship practices.

TRIMESTER 4

Theology 4: Advanced topics in systematic theology, focusing on contemporary theological issues.
This course delves into advanced topics in systematic theology with a focus on contemporary theological issues, such as the relationship between theology and culture, interfaith dialogue, and ethical challenges in modern society.

Hermeneutics: Principles and methods of biblical interpretation.
This course provides students with the tools for sound biblical interpretation, emphasizing the importance of context, genre, and theological coherence.

Introduction to Biblical Theology: Overview of the unifying themes and theological trajectory of the Bible.
This foundational course explores the unifying themes of the Bible, tracing the development of redemptive history and theological concepts across the canon.

Historical Books: Study of the historical books of the Old Testament, their narratives, and theological themes.
A focused study on the historical books of the Old Testament, examining their narrative content, historical context, and theological contributions to the biblical canon.

Christian Counseling: Principles and practices of counseling from a Christian perspective.
This course equips students with a biblical framework for counseling, addressing the integration of theology and psychology, and practical methods for ministry.

TRIMESTER 5

Research Method & Writing: Training in academic research, writing, and presentation of theological papers.
This course equips students with the skills needed for academic research, theological writing, and effective presentation of theological arguments. Emphasis is placed on clarity, accuracy, and adherence to Reformed theological principles.

Church Planting & Growth: Strategies for planting new churches and fostering church growth.
This course explores strategies for planting and nurturing new churches, with a focus on biblical principles and contextual adaptations.

Pastoral Counseling: Advanced counseling techniques for pastoral care.
This course offers advanced techniques for pastoral care, focusing on the practical application of biblical wisdom to real-life challenges.

Homiletics 1: Introduction to the art and science of preaching, focusing on sermon preparation.
This introductory course focuses on the art and science of sermon preparation, exploring the theological and practical dimensions of preaching.

Gospels: Study of the four Gospels, focusing on the life and teachings of Jesus Christ.
An in-depth study of the four Gospels, focusing on the life, ministry, and teachings of Jesus Christ as central to redemptive history.

Christian Worldview: Examination of how a biblical worldview shapes thinking and living.
This course examines how a biblical worldview shapes thinking, ethics, and decision-making, addressing the implications for various spheres of life.

Pastoral Theology: Study of the pastoral role, including care, counseling, and leadership within the church.
This course explores the theological foundations and practical responsibilities of pastoral ministry, with an emphasis on care, counseling, and leadership.

TRIMESTER 6

Catechism / Calvinism: Study of catechetical instruction, focusing on Calvinist doctrine and its application.
This course explores catechetical instruction within the Reformed tradition, focusing on Calvinist theology, key doctrines, and their practical application in the life of the church and the believer.

Christian Ethics: Study of moral theology, including ethical issues from a Christian perspective.
A study of moral theology from a Christian perspective, this course addresses ethical dilemmas in contemporary life, emphasizing biblical principles and their practical application.

Pentateuch: Detailed study of the first five books of the Old Testament, their theology, and covenantal significance.
This course provides an in-depth study of the first five books of the Old Testament, exploring their historical, theological, and covenantal significance within the context of redemptive history.

Apologetics: Defense of the Christian faith against objections and alternative worldviews.
This course focuses on defending the Christian faith against objections and engaging alternative worldviews with grace and truth, grounded in Reformed theology.

Pastoral Ethics: Study of ethical issues specifically related to pastoral ministry.
This course examines ethical issues specific to pastoral ministry, addressing challenges and providing guidance for maintaining integrity and faithfulness in leadership.

World Mission: Study of the biblical basis for mission, history of missions, and contemporary mission strategies.
This course examines the biblical foundation for missions, the history of global mission efforts, and contemporary strategies for cross-cultural evangelism and church planting.

Leadership Development: Training in leadership principles and their application in ministry contexts.
This course provides training in leadership principles, emphasizing their practical application in ministry contexts to build and lead effective teams.

ADDITIONAL - ELECTIVE SUBJECTS

Revelations: In-depth study of the Book of Revelation, its interpretations, and eschatological themes.
An in-depth study of the Book of Revelation, focusing on its historical context, interpretive approaches, and eschatological themes, with an emphasis on its relevance to the Reformed faith.

Poetical Books: Study of the wisdom literature and poetic books of the Old Testament.
This course explores the wisdom and poetic books of the Old Testament, focusing on their literary beauty, theological depth, and practical relevance.

General Epistles: Examination of the non-Pauline epistles, their themes, and theological significance. This course examines the non-Pauline epistles (James, 1–2 Peter, 1–3 John, Jude), emphasizing their historical context, key themes, and theological contributions.

Historical Books: Continued study of the Old Testament historical books, focusing on different narratives.
A continuation of the study of Old Testament historical books, focusing on narratives and theological lessons relevant to God’s covenant faithfulness and human responsibility.

Hebrew 1: An introductory course in biblical Hebrew, providing foundational skills for reading and interpreting the Old Testament in its original language.

Homiletics 2: A continuation of Homiletics 1, this course deepens the student’s understanding of sermon preparation and delivery, with a focus on advanced techniques and specialized preaching contexts.
